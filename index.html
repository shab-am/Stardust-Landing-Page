Explore galaxies

Andromeda Galaxy (M31)

The Andromeda Galaxy is our nearest spiral galaxy, located about 2.537 million light-years away. It's slightly larger than the Milky Way and is expected to collide with our galaxy in about 4.5 billion years.

Know more

Milky Way Galaxy

Our home galaxy, the Milky Way, is a barred spiral galaxy containing our Solar System. It's estimated to be about 100,000 light-years in diameter and contains billions of stars.

Know more

Whirlpool Galaxy (M51)

This grand-design spiral galaxy exhibits a prominent spiral structure and interacts with a smaller galaxy, creating a spectacular visual bridge of stars, gas, and dust.

Know more

Triangulum Galaxy (M33)

The Triangulum Galaxy is a spiral galaxy and a member of the Local Group, situated about 3 million light-years away. It's the third-largest galaxy in the group after Andromeda and the Milky Way.

Know more

Pinwheel Galaxy (M101)

Also known as the "Pinwheel Nebula," it's a face-on spiral galaxy located in the constellation Ursa Major. It showcases bright spiral arms rich in star-forming regions.

Know more

Cigar Galaxy (M82)

This irregular galaxy has an unusual appearance due to its vigorous star formation, resulting from interactions with its neighbor, the Bode's Galaxy (M81).

Know more

Cartwheel Galaxy

Known for its unique ring-like appearance, the Cartwheel Galaxy is the result of a galactic collision, producing a shockwave of star formation.

Know more

Messier 87 (M87)

An elliptical galaxy in the Virgo Cluster, M87 is notable for containing a supermassive black hole at its center, imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope in 2019.

Know more

Sculptor Galaxy (NGC 253)

This starburst galaxy, located in the constellation Sculptor, is characterized by its dusty spiral arms and intense star-forming activity.

Know more

Sombrero Galaxy (M104)

Known for its prominent bulge and dust lane, the Sombrero Galaxy is an unbarred spiral galaxy located in the constellation Virgo. Its appearance resembles the shape of a Mexican hat.
